This Ocean Buries Xu Lizhi

By Chen Nianxi

Following the sound of waves I arrived at this body of water
A person who has never been to the sea before
knows well what the ocean buries
The ocean can’t be demolished, but it can be polluted
like a modern history of civilization, full of marks of copulation

This vast ocean
Transporting subcontract adidas shoes, sent by steel laborers towards the opposite shore
and confronting blue eyed foreigners’ fortified gun ships
Gun barrels filling up with the gunpowder made by our ancestors
Now it buries a poet and his mechanical youth worth less than his work station
Those twenty four years of an imprisoned soul lie here
is it given freedom?

From Suiyang I set out hitchhiking
Hurriedly arriving at Zunyi airport, hitching a ride again in a giant beetle car to Shenzhen
I came to the ocean under the scorching sun, not arriving here to pay a visit
These years I have seen too many youths wither and die
They died in the ocean, died in the mountains, died on their way to a cold breeze and bright moon
Neither youth nor poem can change the filth and blue of the ocean
I am merely here to complete some parts of this life’s journey
Watching how a seagull flies on a frigid June day

Where the sky meets the ocean
there is an invisible island
It is said in some years there once lived an immortal
now there lives an industrial boat
Big and tall palm trees wave on the waterfront
The turning clock has already gone amiss for many years
In this poem, Chen Nian-Xi uses the suicide of fellow migrant worker Xu Lizhi to denounce the globalization and industrialization of both China and the world that has led to the suffering and death of so many laborers. While Chen Nian-Xi blames the ocean directly, the true responsibility falls on the societies that have pushed this kind of factory-work and global commerce forward. Chen Nian-Xi writes of the confrontation between Chinese laborers and militant westerners caused by transnational trade:
This vast ocean
Transporting adidas shoes, made by iron laborers, to the opposite shore
and bringing in blue eyed foreigners’ fortified gun ships
Gun barrels filling up with the gunpowder made by our ancestors
Now it buries a poet and his mechanical youth worth less than his work station
Those twenty four years of an imprisoned soul lie here
is it given freedom?
This stanza illustrates what the ocean has beared witness to over hundreds of years, connecting the violence of the Opium Wars as well as China's rapid industrialization to Xu Lizhi's suicide. Chen Nian-Xi points to the gunpowder, created by the Chinese, yet eventually stolen and used by Westerners in a conquest of Asia. Focusing on Xu Lizhi, Chen Nianxi calls out the deceased worker’s lack of perceived value in society. Chen Nian-Xi asserts that for all twenty-four years of his life, Xu Lizhi has been trapped within this society, his soul confined to the factory. In the concluding line, Chen Nian-Xi goes further to critique that even in death, Xu Lizhi’s soul may not be able to find freedom. With this critique, Chen Nian-Xi condemns both the society and the world.
